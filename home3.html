History Of Periodic Table

1789: Antoine Lavoisier's Periodic Table

In 1789, Antoine Lavoisier, known as the "Father of Modern Chemistry," made the first scientific attempt to classify the chemical elements. In his book "Traité Élémentaire de Chimie" he listed 33 substances he considered elements, grouped into four categories: gases, non-metals, metals, and earthy substances. His work laid the foundation for future periodic classification.

Lavoisier's list of "simple substances".

1829: Döbereiner's Triads

In 1829, Johann Wolfgang Döbereiner proposed that certain elements could be grouped in sets of three (triads) where the atomic mass of the middle element was approximately the average of the other two. This was the first attempt to show a relationship between atomic masses and properties.

Example: Chlorine, Bromine, and Iodine triad.

1869: Mendeleev's Periodic Table

In 1869, Dmitri Mendeleev arranged 63 known elements by increasing atomic mass, grouping them by chemical properties. He left blank spaces for undiscovered elements and predicted their properties with remarkable accuracy — a hallmark of scientific theory.

Mendeleev's original table from 1869.

1913: The Modern Periodic Table

After Henry Moseley's 1913 discovery of atomic number, the table was reorganized by proton count rather than mass. The modern periodic table has 7 periods (rows) and 18 groups (columns), correctly placing all 118 known elements.

The modern table, ordered by atomic number.
